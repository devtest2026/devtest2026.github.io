Building a DEX in 2025: AMMs, Order Books & Hybrid Models

Nov 28, 2024 11 min read PumaPulse Engineering Team
← Back to Blog

Decentralized exchanges processed over $1 trillion in volume in 2024, and the architectural decisions you make at the start will define your DEX's performance, security, and user experience for years. Choosing between an AMM, on-chain order book, or hybrid model is the most critical decision — and the right answer depends on your target users, chain, and liquidity strategy.

Key Takeaways

  • AMMs (Uniswap-style) are the fastest path to launch and always liquid, but suffer from impermanent loss and high slippage on large trades — best for long-tail token pairs.
  • Concentrated liquidity (Uniswap v3 model) dramatically improves capital efficiency but requires active LP management — passive LPs often underperform compared to v2.
  • On-chain order books deliver CEX-quality price discovery but require a high-throughput chain (Solana, Arbitrum) to be economically viable — gas costs kill the model on Ethereum mainnet.
  • Smart contract audits are non-negotiable: DEX contracts hold user funds and are immutable once deployed. Budget $50k–$150k for a thorough audit before mainnet launch.

AMM Architecture

Automated Market Makers use liquidity pools and mathematical formulas to price assets without a traditional order book. The original constant product formula (x*y=k, popularized by Uniswap v2) is elegant in its simplicity: the product of the two token reserves must remain constant after every trade. This means liquidity is always available at some price, making AMMs permissionless and always-on — any token pair can be listed without approval, and trades execute 24/7 without market makers.

The tradeoff is impermanent loss — the opportunity cost LPs pay when the price ratio of their deposited tokens changes. If you deposit ETH/USDC at a 1:2000 ratio and ETH doubles to $4000, you would have been better off simply holding ETH. The AMM rebalances your position toward the cheaper asset as arbitrageurs exploit the price discrepancy. For stable pairs (USDC/USDT) or correlated assets, impermanent loss is minimal. For volatile pairs, it can significantly erode LP returns, especially in trending markets.

Uniswap v3's concentrated liquidity model addresses capital efficiency by allowing LPs to provide liquidity within specific price ranges rather than across the entire 0-to-infinity curve. An LP who believes ETH will trade between $2,800 and $3,200 can concentrate all their capital in that range, earning fees as if they had 10–100x more capital in a v2 pool. The downside: if price moves outside the range, the LP holds 100% of the cheaper asset and earns zero fees until price returns. This makes v3 LP positions behave more like active options strategies than passive income.

On-Chain Order Book DEX

On-chain order books match buyers and sellers like a centralized exchange but settle every trade on-chain. dYdX (on StarkEx), Serum (on Solana), and Hyperliquid have demonstrated that this model is viable at scale when built on the right infrastructure. The advantages are significant: better price discovery, zero slippage for limit orders, familiar UX for traders coming from CEXs, and no impermanent loss for liquidity providers who act as market makers.

The fundamental challenge is gas cost. On Ethereum mainnet, placing, canceling, and filling orders each cost gas — making high-frequency market making economically impossible. This is why on-chain order books only work on high-throughput, low-fee chains. Solana (65,000 TPS, sub-cent fees) is the natural home for on-chain order books. Arbitrum and Optimism make it viable for lower-frequency trading. The matching engine itself can be implemented as a smart contract (fully decentralized but slower) or as an off-chain sequencer with on-chain settlement (faster but introduces a trusted component).

Front-running is a critical concern for on-chain order books. Since all pending transactions are visible in the mempool, sophisticated bots can insert transactions ahead of large orders to profit at the expense of regular users. Solutions include commit-reveal schemes (submit a hash of your order first, reveal later), private mempools (Flashbots Protect, MEV Blocker), and frequent batch auctions (CoW Protocol's approach) where all orders in a batch are settled at a single clearing price, eliminating front-running within the batch.

Hybrid Approaches

The most sophisticated DEXs in 2025 use hybrid architectures that combine the best of both worlds. The most common pattern is off-chain order matching with on-chain settlement: an off-chain sequencer matches orders at high speed (milliseconds) and batches the settlements on-chain. This gives users CEX-like execution speed while maintaining non-custodial settlement. dYdX v4 (on its own Cosmos appchain), Vertex Protocol, and Drift Protocol all use variants of this approach.

Another powerful hybrid is the "just-in-time" (JIT) liquidity model, where professional market makers provide concentrated liquidity specifically for large trades in the same block. When a large swap is detected in the mempool, a market maker adds concentrated liquidity around the current price, captures the fee from the large trade, and removes the liquidity in the same transaction. This dramatically reduces slippage for large traders while allowing market makers to deploy capital efficiently without the impermanent loss risk of passive LP positions.

Code Example — Uniswap v3 Concentrated Liquidity (Solidity)

function addLiquidity(
    address token0,
    address token1,
    uint24 fee,
    int24 tickLower,   // lower price bound
    int24 tickUpper,   // upper price bound
    uint256 amount0Desired,
    uint256 amount1Desired
) external returns (uint256 tokenId, uint128 liquidity) {
    // Mint a new position NFT representing the LP position
    (tokenId, liquidity,,) = positionManager.mint(
        INonfungiblePositionManager.MintParams({
            token0: token0, token1: token1, fee: fee,
            tickLower: tickLower, tickUpper: tickUpper,
            amount0Desired: amount0Desired,
            amount1Desired: amount1Desired,
            amount0Min: 0, amount1Min: 0,
            recipient: msg.sender,
            deadline: block.timestamp + 300
        })
    );
}

Smart Contract Security

DEX smart contracts are immutable once deployed and hold user funds — making security the highest-stakes engineering challenge in the entire stack. The most common attack vectors are reentrancy (a malicious contract calls back into your contract before state is updated), flash loan exploits (borrowing millions in a single transaction to manipulate prices), and oracle manipulation (using your DEX's own price as an oracle, which can be manipulated in a single block). The DAO hack ($60M), Uniswap v1 reentrancy vulnerability, and dozens of AMM oracle exploits all stem from these patterns.

Mandatory security practices: use OpenZeppelin's ReentrancyGuard on all state-changing functions, use time-weighted average prices (TWAPs) rather than spot prices for any oracle functionality, implement circuit breakers that pause the contract if unusual activity is detected, and use a proxy pattern (UUPS or Transparent Proxy) to allow emergency upgrades if a critical vulnerability is discovered post-launch. For price oracles, Chainlink or Pyth Network are far safer than using your own pool's spot price.

Audit strategy matters as much as the audit itself. A single audit from one firm is insufficient — different auditors catch different classes of bugs. Budget for at least two independent audits from reputable firms (Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, Certik, Spearbit). Run a public bug bounty on Immunefi with meaningful rewards ($50k–$500k depending on TVL) before and after launch. Consider a formal verification of your core invariants using tools like Certora Prover or Halmos. The cost of a thorough security program ($100k–$300k) is trivial compared to the reputational and financial damage of a hack.

Choosing Your Chain

Chain selection is a strategic decision that shapes your entire user base, liquidity access, and technical architecture. Ethereum mainnet offers maximum liquidity, composability with the entire DeFi ecosystem, and the most battle-tested smart contract environment — but gas costs make it unsuitable for high-frequency trading or small transactions. Ethereum is best for large-value DeFi protocols where security and composability outweigh cost concerns.

Arbitrum and Optimism give you Ethereum security with 10–100x lower gas costs and EVM equivalence — meaning you can deploy your Ethereum contracts with minimal changes. Arbitrum has the largest L2 TVL and the most active DeFi ecosystem. Solana offers the highest throughput (65,000 TPS) and lowest fees, making it the only viable chain for on-chain order books and high-frequency trading applications. The tradeoff is a different programming model (Rust/Anchor instead of Solidity) and a less mature DeFi ecosystem.

Multi-chain deployment is increasingly the right answer for protocols targeting broad adoption. Deploy your core contracts on 2–3 chains and use a cross-chain bridge (LayerZero, Wormhole, or Axelar) to enable liquidity sharing and unified user experience. The operational complexity is significant — you're now managing deployments, monitoring, and incident response across multiple chains — but the user reach and liquidity benefits often justify it for protocols past product-market fit.
