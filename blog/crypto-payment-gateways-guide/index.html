Crypto Payment Gateways: The Complete Integration Guide

Nov 8, 2024 10 min read PumaPulse Engineering Team
← Back to Blog

Accepting cryptocurrency payments opens your business to a global, borderless customer base — no chargebacks, no currency conversion friction, and settlement in minutes rather than days. But integrating crypto payments correctly requires careful thought about architecture, volatility management, compliance, and accounting. This guide covers everything you need to go from zero to production.

Key Takeaways

  • Generate a unique wallet address per transaction — never reuse addresses. This is the foundation of a correct payment architecture and enables reliable transaction tracking.
  • For most merchants, instant fiat conversion at payment time is the right default — it eliminates volatility risk and simplifies accounting without requiring crypto treasury management expertise.
  • Every crypto payment is a taxable event in most jurisdictions. Your system must record the fiat value at time of receipt from day one — retrofitting this later is painful and error-prone.
  • AML screening on incoming transactions (via Chainalysis or Elliptic) is increasingly expected by regulators and banking partners — implement it before you scale, not after.

Choosing a Gateway

The gateway decision comes down to build vs. buy, and the right answer depends on your volume, technical resources, and control requirements. Off-the-shelf gateways like BitPay, Coinbase Commerce, and NOWPayments handle the entire stack — wallet generation, blockchain monitoring, confirmation handling, and fiat settlement — in exchange for fees of 0.5–1% per transaction. They're the fastest path to accepting crypto payments and require minimal engineering investment.

For high-volume merchants (processing $1M+/month), the economics shift dramatically in favor of building a custom gateway. At 1% fees, you're paying $10,000/month for infrastructure that costs a fraction of that to operate. Custom gateways also give you full control over supported currencies, settlement timing, and the user experience. The engineering investment is significant — 2–4 months for a production-ready system — but the long-term savings and flexibility justify it at scale.

Key evaluation criteria beyond fees: supported cryptocurrencies (does it include the coins your customers want to use?), settlement options (crypto or fiat, and in which currencies?), API quality and documentation, webhook reliability, and compliance features (KYC/AML screening, sanctions checking). For B2B payments, also evaluate support for stablecoins (USDT, USDC) — many business customers prefer stablecoins to avoid volatility entirely.

Integration Architecture

A production crypto payment gateway has four core components. First, a wallet address generator that creates a unique deposit address for every transaction — this is critical for reliable payment attribution. Use HD (hierarchical deterministic) wallets with BIP-44 derivation paths so you can generate millions of addresses from a single seed phrase. Never reuse addresses across transactions, as this makes it impossible to reliably attribute payments to specific orders.

Second, a blockchain monitor that watches for incoming transactions to your generated addresses. For Bitcoin and Ethereum, you can run your own full node or use a provider like Alchemy, Infura, or QuickNode. The monitor should detect unconfirmed transactions immediately (for UX purposes) but only trigger payment confirmation after a sufficient number of block confirmations — typically 1–3 for Ethereum, 3–6 for Bitcoin, depending on the payment amount and your risk tolerance.

Third, a settlement engine that converts received crypto to fiat (if desired) and credits the merchant's account. For instant conversion, integrate with a crypto exchange API (Coinbase Advanced, Kraken, or Binance) to sell the received crypto immediately. For crypto-native merchants who want to hold, the settlement engine simply moves funds to the merchant's designated wallet. Fourth, a webhook system that notifies your backend of payment events in real time — payment detected, confirmed, settled, or failed.

Code Example — Payment Address Generation (Node.js / ethers.js)

const { ethers } = require('ethers');

class PaymentAddressGenerator {
  constructor(masterSeed) {
    // BIP-44 HD wallet: m/44'/60'/0'/0/index
    this.hdNode = ethers.HDNodeWallet.fromPhrase(masterSeed);
  }

  generateAddress(orderId, index) {
    const child = this.hdNode.derivePath(`m/44'/60'/0'/0/${index}`);
    return {
      address: child.address,
      orderId,
      index,
      createdAt: Date.now(),
      expiresAt: Date.now() + 15 * 60 * 1000  // 15 min window
    };
  }
}

Handling Volatility

Crypto price volatility is the most common concern merchants raise when considering crypto payments. A customer pays 0.05 ETH for a $100 product, and by the time you settle, ETH has dropped 10% — you've effectively received $90. The standard solution is instant conversion: the moment a payment is confirmed, your gateway sells the received crypto on an exchange and credits your account in fiat. This eliminates volatility risk entirely and makes crypto payments behave like any other payment method from an accounting perspective.

Price locking is an alternative for merchants who want to offer a better UX without instant conversion. When a customer initiates payment, you lock the exchange rate for a short window (typically 10–15 minutes) and display the exact crypto amount required. If the customer pays within the window, you honor the locked rate. If they miss the window, the invoice expires and they must restart with the current rate. This approach requires you to absorb short-term volatility risk, which is manageable for small amounts but can be significant for large transactions.

Stablecoin-first strategies are increasingly popular for B2B payments. By accepting USDT or USDC instead of BTC or ETH, you eliminate volatility entirely without needing instant conversion infrastructure. Stablecoins settle in minutes, have near-zero fees on networks like Polygon or Arbitrum, and are increasingly accepted by suppliers and contractors — making them a complete alternative to traditional wire transfers for many business payment flows.

Tax & Accounting

In most jurisdictions, receiving crypto as payment is a taxable event — you must recognize income equal to the fair market value of the crypto at the time of receipt. This means your payment system must record the USD (or local currency) value of every incoming transaction at the exact moment it's received. Failing to do this from day one creates a painful retroactive accounting problem, as you'll need to reconstruct historical prices for every transaction.

If you hold received crypto rather than converting immediately, you also have a capital gains event when you eventually sell — the gain or loss is calculated from the value at time of receipt to the value at time of sale. For businesses processing significant crypto volume, this creates complex tax reporting requirements. Crypto-specific accounting tools like Koinly, TaxBit, or Cryptio can automate much of this by connecting to your wallets and exchanges and generating the required tax reports. For high-volume operations, integrating directly with your accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero, NetSuite) via API is worth the engineering investment.

Compliance Requirements

Accepting crypto payments may trigger regulatory obligations depending on your jurisdiction and business model. In the US, if you're converting crypto to fiat on behalf of customers, you may be operating as a Money Services Business (MSB) and need to register with FinCEN. In the EU, the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation creates new obligations for crypto payment service providers. The safest approach is to consult a crypto-specialized attorney before launching, as the regulatory landscape varies significantly by country and is evolving rapidly.

AML screening on incoming transactions is increasingly expected by regulators and banking partners. Services like Chainalysis KYT (Know Your Transaction) and Elliptic analyze the blockchain history of incoming funds and flag transactions associated with sanctioned addresses, darknet markets, or other high-risk sources. Implementing this screening before you scale is far easier than retrofitting it later — and it protects you from inadvertently receiving proceeds of crime, which can trigger asset freezes and regulatory investigations.
